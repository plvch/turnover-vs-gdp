Title: **The curious structure of German exports**
Voice: knowledgeable explainer with bite; first-person curiosity; no em dashes.
Figures per SPINE.md. VIZ markers inline. Final text-style polish + methods note pending.

---

Germany's place in the world economy gets argued through one number. Exports: about
1.56 trillion euros of goods in 2023, the surplus, the "export champion" badge, the
standing fear that the whole model rests on foreigners buying Volkswagens and machine
tools. It is a real number. It is also a narrow window onto what German firms actually
do, and the view through the rest of the house is stranger. This is what I found when I
went looking, in the order I found it.

Start with a number I had managed never to notice: the total taxable turnover of German
business. It crossed eight trillion euros in 2021 and now runs close to nine. Twice GDP.
Five times exports. A figure that big, and it never makes the speeches, the headlines, or
the books about German decline.

What is it, though. Easy to read more into it than it deserves. Turnover is not output,
it is transactions: every sale, counted in full, every time a thing changes hands. Steel
to the parts maker, parts to the carmaker, car to the dealer. Four sales, four entries,
one car. GDP is the disciplined cousin that counts only the value each step actually
adds. That is the whole story of why turnover runs to twice GDP. The doubling is not
wealth hiding somewhere; it is the same euros counted again at every handoff, and the
size of the gap tells you how long the chains are. [VIZ 1]

The multiplier is boringly stable, a hair either side of two, year after year. The
interest is in the wobble. Because turnover drags the full price of everything through
every stage, it feels prices far more than GDP does, and it lurches about twice as hard.
And it lurches on prices, not on real activity. 2009: oil and trade fell out of the sky,
turnover dropped nearly ten percent, GDP under four. 2022: energy detonated, turnover
jumped fifteen, GDP eight. 2020, the pandemic, a real shock with quiet prices, and the
two lines barely noticed each other. Turnover is not a scoreboard. It is a pressure
gauge. [VIZ 2]

Bring exports back in. Eight trillion of turnover at home against 1.56 trillion shipped
abroad means the domestic churn is roughly five times the cash the export champion
actually exports. The number the country frets about is a sliver of the trade German
companies do.

Which begs the obvious question, the one that sent me down this hole. If turnover is
every transaction a German firm makes, is it everything a German firm does? You would
assume yes. It is not even close. There is a whole storey of the building that turnover,
GDP and the trade balance all walk straight past. Trade economists live there. Almost
nobody else has been shown the room.

It has a dull name, Foreign Affiliate Statistics, and a loud content: what German-owned
companies do outside Germany. The turnover and the payroll of every plant, subsidiary and
joint venture they own abroad. None of it touches German GDP. None of it touches the
eight trillion. In 2023 these foreign affiliates turned over 3.4 trillion euros and
employed 6.7 million people. Forty percent of domestic turnover. Eighty percent of GDP. A
second German economy, nearly the size of the one we measure, sitting off the books the
country fights about. [VIZ 3]

One firm shows the mechanism. A German carmaker, a generation ago: a full-stack factory
at home, design and engineering and assembly in one place, and the only thing that
crossed the border was a finished car booked as an export. The same firm today has been
pulled apart. The brain stays home: design, engineering, the hard components, the
patents, the headquarters. The hands move to a plant abroad that the firm still owns
outright. Now three different things cross the border, and the statistics treat them as
strangers. Hard parts and materials, made in Germany, counted as exports, the jobs at
home. Engineering and IP, invoiced to the foreign plant from a German office. And, more
and more, the finished car itself, built and sold in Tennessee or Hefei, in no German
trade figure at all. [VIZ 5: the anatomy, every box tagged visible or invisible to German GDP]

The accounts take this single, tightly integrated company and slice it down the border.
Everything on the home side lands in GDP, in turnover, in the export tallies. Everything
on the far side shows up only in Foreign Affiliate Statistics, inside someone else's GDP.
One machine, filed as half a firm.

Two things about this hidden storey are worth the climb.

First, it has been growing faster than every headline number. From 2010 to 2023, turnover
abroad compounded at about 5.7 percent a year, headcount abroad at 2.8. Domestic turnover
managed 3.8, exports 3.9, GDP roughly three, domestic jobs under one. The foreign economy
outpaced every figure the decline-of-Germany genre is built on, the same figures filling
the gloomier books on the shelf right now. What it traces is an up-market migration of
both product and labour: keep the engineering, export the manufacturing. The macro
statistics have no column for it. [VIZ 4]

Second, the geography, and here the United States is the cleanest example. German goods
exports to America in 2023 came to 157.9 billion euros, the single biggest market, the
number that gets a tariff threat attached to it. German-owned operations inside America
turned over around 760 billion, very nearly five times more. The export figure wildly
undersells how much German economy is actually standing on US soil, because most of it is
built and sold there. Which is, if anything, the friendlier story: real local operations,
local payrolls, local tax bills, not the one-way flood the trade-deficit crowd keeps
drawing.

And then the twist that makes it genuinely strange. For all that scale, barely any of the
foreign economy comes home as money. German firms earned about 158 billion euros on their
foreign operations in 2023. Half went out as dividends, much of the rest was plowed
straight back in abroad. Net of what foreign owners pulled out of Germany, the national
accounts logged maybe 70 billion. Against 3.4 trillion of turnover, that is under five
percent, about 1.7 percent of GDP, a rounding error. Even national income, the one
measure built to catch what a country earns abroad, only ever sees that thin slice of
profit, never the activity beneath it. The pie is real, it is legal, and it sits
permanently outside the tax base and the GDP and the surplus. What stays visible at home
is not the money. It is the shape of the work the foreign scale keeps alive: the
engineering centres, the head offices, the design floors that only pencil out because
someone is building the volume somewhere cheaper.

So that is the curious structure of German exports. GDP, exports, turnover, the numbers
the country watches, all describe the economy you can see. The economy German firms
increasingly run, and the comfortable home jobs it quietly underwrites, sits outside all
of them. The statistics are territorial. The firm stopped being territorial a long time
ago.
