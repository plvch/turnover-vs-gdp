Title - The curious structure of German exports. 

## The arc

**§1 — Hook: the €8tn nobody mentions.**
Coming out of the small-business / micro-business posts, the thing that stuck was the
sheer size of the turnover figure — ~€8tn — a number never heard in ordinary talk
about the German economy. Start there: a number this big that nobody cites.

**§2 — What it actually is (explainer + VIZ 1).**
Turnover is a *sum of transactions* — the total of everything companies buy and sell to
build all goods and services, counted at every hop. Not value created (that's GDP), but
value *moved*. VIZ 1: turnover-vs-GDP explainer — gross output (counts every stage) vs
value added (counts each stage's margin once). The ~2x gap is double-counting, not magic.

**§3 — The stats of it (VIZ 2).**
Generally a **~2x multiplier** (turnover ≈ 2.0x GDP, stable 1.9-2.1). The interesting part
is the variation: turnover is ~2x as volatile and amplifies *price* shocks (2009 oil -9.5%
vs GDP -3.6%; 2022 energy +15% vs +8%; 2020 COVID barely diverged — a real not price shock).
VIZ 2: turnover & GDP YoY over time, or the ratio line with the price-shock spikes.

**§4 — The export pivot (the hinge).**
So turnover is **~5x the exports** (€8tn vs €1.56tn goods exports). Technically true: the
domestic transactions of the economy are ~5x the cash the "export champion" actually ships.
Then the question that opens the door: if turnover is *all* the transactions, does it capture
*everything* German companies do and impact? Apparently **not at all** — there's a shadow
metric, well known to trade economists, basically unknown to the general public (who picture
countries by import/export quotas). Bridge to FATS.

**§5 — FATS: the invisible economy (VIZ 3, VIZ 4).**
What German companies turn over and employ *abroad* — completely invisible in the GDP
conversation. (And going ahead - the GNI too if we go that road). VIZ 3: the known (GDP / domestic turnover) next to the less-known "ghost"
turnover abroad.
- **(a) It's big.** €3.4tn abroad ≈ **40% of domestic turnover** (~80% of GDP), living
  entirely outside the conversation.
- **(worked example — VIZ 5, firm anatomy).** Make it concrete with one firm (a
  carmaker). BEFORE: a full-stack factory at home; the finished car crosses the border
  as an export. AFTER: the value chain is unbundled. Stay HOME (visible to GDP /
  domestic turnover / exports / jobs): design, engineering, IP, HQ/offices, hardest
  components and materials. Move ABROAD (visible only in FATS + host GDP, invisible to
  German GDP): assembly + volume manufacturing in a firm-owned plant. The finished car
  increasingly built+sold abroad, in NO German trade data. One integrated firm, cut in
  half at the border by the accounts. Annotate each piece visible vs invisible to GDP.
- **(b) It grew faster than every headline (VIZ 4).** Abroad turnover +5.7%/yr and abroad
  employment +2.8%/yr (2010-23) — faster than GDP, exports (~+3.9%), domestic turnover
  (+3.8%), domestic employment (+0.9%) — i.e. faster than the very numbers the "doomer"
  German-decline headlines cite. Reading: an up-market move of *products* mirrored by an
  up-market move of *labour* — engineering here, manufacturing there — a structural shift
  that sits outside conventional macro. [HONESTY: argued interpretation, not a regression.]
- **(c) The US case.** German official exports to the US (€157.9bn) are ~**5x smaller** than
  German-owned US operations' turnover (€760bn). The German economy's presence inside US GDP
  is far larger than the trade figure implies — more than generally known, and arguably more positive to the protectionist angle - it's legitimate operations with local jobs and everything. 
- **(d) The twist: on cash, it barely comes home.** Profits are largely reinvested abroad;
  net direct-investment income ~€70bn (distributed ~€99bn), <5% of the €3.4tn, ~1.7% of GDP.
  The pie legitimately stays outside tax/GDP statistics. What you *can* see at home is only
  the *structure* of the work — the engineering/HQ/design the foreign scale sustains.

## Closing note
The headline metrics (GDP, exports, turnover) describe the economy you can see. The thing
that increasingly shapes it — the foreign economy German firms run, and the home jobs it
underwrites — is invisible to all of them. The statistics are territorial; the firm is not.

## Numbers locked (see SPINE.md for sources)
- Turnover ~€8tn (Veranlagungen 2021 8.12tn; Voranmeldungen 2024 8.78tn). ~2.0x GDP.
- Turnover / goods exports (1.56tn) ~= 5x. GDP 2023 ~4.2tn.
- Abroad (FATS) 2023 = 3.39tn, 6.7M employees; = 40% of domestic turnover, ~80% of GDP.
- CAGR 2010-23: abroad turnover +5.7%, abroad empl +2.8%; domestic turnover +3.8%,
  exports ~+3.9%, domestic empl +0.9%.
- US: affiliate turnover 760bn vs exports 157.9bn = 4.81x.
- Cash home: net DI income 70.1bn (gross 158.0bn; distributed 99.0; reinvested abroad 34.8).
- 2021 break (financial-sector ~692bn) flagged wherever 2021 turnover appears.
